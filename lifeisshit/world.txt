### Chapter 1: LILIT, TAKE MY HAND

The peephole went black for a second. Then a key snarled in the lock.  
Yakov swung the door open. The prick was all tuxed up, bow-tie and everything.  

— Ah… It’s you…  
— You’re damn right it’s me, — I said. — How’s it hanging?  
— Mm-hmm.  

Yakov eyeballed my feet.

— Shoes off. Look at you… You’ll track mud everywhere, — Yakov grumbled. — I know you don’t give a shit, but I’m the one scrubbing up.

Rain was dumping outside; I was soaked head to toe.  
— Get in already, — Yakov said. — Everybody’s here. Even Peter. — He smirked.

— How would I know… — Yakov shrugged. — Says he’s got a “feeling.”

Classic Yakov: obsessed with spotless floors and that thick-headed bootlicking for the Teacher — bootlicking laced with envy, dark, dull, and gray as fuck.  

I hung up my coat, yanked off my shoes and soggy socks, and crossed the creaky parquet into the sitting room.  

— Peace to this house! — I scanned the crew.  

Yeah, everyone was here. Thomas sat off to the side, sneering like he was above it all. Andrew, as always, was meek and mute. And Mary… Mary slept softly on the couch; my eyes lingered on her a beat too long. Then I turned to Peter — true to form: a flashy, trashy dress, wig, cigarette pinched delicately between manicured fingers. His face gave away zilch — no joy, no worry. Silence.  
“Answer, Peter, I’m waiting.” — Yeshu locked eyes with him. — “You know her soul? If yes, step right up, take my place, lead us your own way — I’ll be the first to follow.”

The gentle giant had monstrous strength; once, fleeing pursuers, he’d knocked out two thugs bare-handed. I made sure to stay on his good side.  

I wriggled free carefully, went to the table, poured a drink.  

— Rotten weather, eh? — came Yeshu’s voice behind me; clearly irritated.  
— Yeah — nasty stuff. I’m covered in shit.

Alright. Got it. No time to argue. Best to filter every word.  

— Ordinary water, — Yeshu repeated. — Same as the tap, only cleaner. If it feels like shit, maybe the problem is you.  
— Me? — I couldn’t help it. — Why me?

Jan listened wide-eyed.  
— Right, — I muttered.  
— Water softens a man, my friend Judas, — Yeshu lectured. — What piles up all year becomes a flood in autumn — only instead of ice it’s shards of your soul. Moral? — The Teacher looked around.  
— Moral?! — Jan blurted, impatient.  

Thomas smirked. Peter pretended not to listen.  

— Simple, — I said. — Leaving your umbrella home on a rainy day is a grave sin.  

Silence settled. Jan shook his head sadly. Peter eyed Yeshu, unsure how to react. Yakov instinctively reached for a broom.  

Yeshu’s gaze fixed on me. In a scarcely audible whisper he said:  

“Lilit, take my hand. Lilit, we’re turning the new page of humankind.”  

A chill ran through me. I wasn’t even sure I’d heard it. Yeshu blinked — as though the moment never happened.  

Then we all heard a strangled little hoot. Yeshu was laughing, then burst into full-throated roaring laughter. The sitting room shook, everyone joined in — everyone except Mary, still asleep, and Jan, who looked around in bewilderment.

### Chapter 2: WATER // SHARDS

When Yeshu launches one of his trademark speeches, it’s hard not to fall under the spell.  
People like him are born when sorrow soaks the earth right through, leaving clots of blood on the surface. Yeshu was one of those clots. However hard I tried, I could never truly fathom him. To call him strange is to say nothing; he seemed woven of pure oddities—yet inside the weave you sensed a kind of twisted, dark order.

Winter or summer he wore the same black jacket and a black beret. Clothes meant nothing to him. The real oddities lived in his character. He spoke slowly, lazily, as if doing you a favor, then suddenly hit you with some blunt, usually tactless question. Refuse to answer and he would flare up. And when Yeshu flared, you stayed the hell out of his way — he could cut you open with one bitter word, though he always apologized afterward.

Humor wasn’t foreign to him either. Once, on our way back from the market, the conversation turned to science.

— All these years, — Yeshu said, — and I still don’t know what the fuck quantum mechanics is.  
— I haven’t got a clue, I admitted.  
— The only thing I’ll swear to is this: it was invented by negroes.  

— Negroes?! — I yelped. — What the hell do negroes have to do with it, Teacher?!  

— What haven’t they got to do with it? — he chuckled. — Negroes invented everything — blues, jazz, human rights, long-distance running… I wouldn’t be surprised if quantum mechanics crawled straight out of their poorhouse too.

He laughed. I saw he was in the mood for a duel of wits and took the bait. Just then a pair of Jews scuttled past us.

— Tell me, Teacher, — I pointed at them, — what could become the future symbol of Zionism?  
— I don’t know. Your suggestion?  
— A circumcised penis, obviously, — I roared, proud of my own genius.  
— Oh friend! A brand new swastika made of pricks and payot.  
— Exactly, — I nodded. — But Teacher, you forgot the noses… So the Jews are planning to enslave the whole globe and a Jewish dictator even worse than Hitler is coming?  
— Quite possible.  
— And what will replace the Aryan salute — the arm thrust to heaven?

Yeshu thought for a second.  
— A mighty erection, of course. A huge circumcised rabbi-cock pointing straight at the sky.  

— Then how the hell do we tell the real Jew from the fake circumcised impostor?  
— A true Jew gets hard not only for a leering wench but for a hundred-dollar bill.

There we go, I thought — he’d seized the initiative again. I tried to wrestle it back:

— So in other words a true Jew gets aroused by that shaggy gray gentleman with frog-eyes bulging?  
— Thus we see: frogs turn a Jew on! — He slapped my shoulder hard.  
— Which means a real Jew is French, I mused. Then I must be brave d’Artagnan and you, Teacher, the silent wise Athos?  
— Yes, yes, — Yeshu nodded, — exactly how the warriors of Charlemagne’s day spoke and acted. A model for every true cavalier.  
— But Teacher! If Jews are French, then who the hell are the French?  
— Well… From what I hear the French come from Algeria, Iraq or Syria. My friends who visited France said it’s full of Arabs.  
— And?  
— Jews and Arabs are the same thing.  
— Ah! Then Sinwar is a wise rabbi?!  
— No, friend — Sinwar’s a Krishnaite.  
— A Krishnaite? But wait, Teacher — “Krishnaite” rhymes with “kike”… there’s something to that. Swear to God, there really is…  
— And “brahmin” rhymes with “rabbi.”  
— Teacher! — I declared triumphantly. — This discovery will make our names immortal!  
— Hold your fame, Judas, hold it right there! — Yeshu waved me down. — Answer this instead: why does the Indian branch of kikes, while shunning beef, shamelessly gobble pork?

I was finished. Completely beaten. Once again he had proven himself a virtuoso orator. I could only sigh.

Yeshu nodded, almost kindly this time.

— Sometimes, — he said, — a completely pointless chat like this helps me survive the gloom. Thank you, Judas.

The rest of the walk home he remained silent.

For all the bursts of laughter that sometimes seized him, he was the saddest man I had ever met — but not with the cheap self-pity of posers. He hated his sadness, fought it desperately — and always lost. Even when he joked, you could feel his heart tearing apart inside his chest.

— A smile, — he loved to repeat, — a plain, simple smile is worth all the tears humanity has ever shed, all its griefs combined.

Yeshu cherished the power he held over us, yet he constantly said he neither wanted nor accepted it — and we would beg him to stay. He saw straight through people, yet could be strangely naïve and trusting, which constantly got him into trouble. Once we found him behind the market — beaten half to death and spat on. It took him a long time to come around, and when he finally did, he flatly refused to say what had happened. After that we tried to send Jan with him whenever possible — the strongest of us. The main thing was to avoid fatal accidents.

We valued him too much.

### Chapter 4: MARY / MUTE / MIRROR

Before she met us, Mary sold fruit in the streets. She came from the edge of town — some rough, torn-up place where the wind always smelled of dust and rotting wood.

From what little she told us, she was about twenty. Her father, Shlomo, was a merchant.  
She told us he used to beat her. With orange crate slats.  
Hard.  
Sometimes for a reason. Sometimes just because the day was long and he was bored.

She looked strange — not ugly, but strange. Black curls. Dark eyes. Skin like chalk.  
A scared child who never quite grew up.

His fists had scrambled something in her head. Not crazy — not fully — but something was off.  
She’d miss the meaning of simple words. Yakov or Peter would slap her for it.  
Quick hands. No patience.

But I’m getting ahead.

It started one morning. Yeshu said he was going to town. We offered to come. He said no.  
Said he wanted to be alone. Didn’t want company.  
It was cold. Even for him.

‘Teacher!’ Jan called after him. ‘Why push us away? Did we do something wrong?’  
Yeshu didn’t answer. Just gave a long, contemptuous stare and walked out.  
He stayed out all day. By evening we were nervous. Started arguing who should go find him. Almost came to blows.  
Then — the doorbell.

‘What’s all the noise?’ Yeshu said, walking in like nothing happened.  
‘Nothing,’ I said. ‘We were… worried.’  
‘Yes, worried!’ Jan shouted. ‘We almost went looking for you!’  

He got a slap for that.  
Hard.  
Yeshu’s eyes burned.  
He breathed slow — forcing the rage down.  
Then said:  
‘Don’t ever do that again.’

After that, his disappearances became routine. He’d rise while we still slept and return when we were tied in knots. Wandering alone he risked his life, but the memory of that slap kept us obedient.

Until one evening he simply didn’t come back. We sat through supper in silence, too scared of his anger to act, too scared of losing him to stay still. Even mighty Jan had been shaken by the slap — what chance had the rest of us? Worse, disobedience could mean banishment — and nobody wanted that.

We drifted to our bunks but nobody slept; we lay waiting for a knock, a key, a footstep. Nothing. Almost till dawn we listened.

Jan broke first — storming round the room, shaking us awake.  
‘Enough lying there! Teacher’s in trouble! Up, damn you!’  
‘Miss the feel of his palm?’ Peter sneered from his bed.  
‘Better a thousand slaps than a lifetime of guilt!’ Jan roared.  
‘Calm down,’ Peter muttered, pulling on his stockings. ‘Nothing will happen to him. If anyone can defend himself, he can.’  
‘Jan’s right!’ Yakov leapt up, dressing fast.  
‘Yes! Yes!’ we all clamoured. Only Thomas stayed silent, picking his teeth.  
‘Coming or not?’ Yakov barked.  
Thomas, unwillingly, hauled himself out.

We found Yeshu at the market on the outskirts, face-down in a pile of rotten fish, fat flies buzzing. He was unconscious, body covered in bruises and cuts. Jan heaved him onto his shoulder. Yeshu’s eyes flickered open for a second.  
‘Teacher!’ Jan muttered, overjoyed.  
‘Don’t leave her…’ Yeshu whispered, voice cracking.  
‘Her? Leave whom?’  
‘Her.’ With inhuman effort he lifted a hand, pointing.  

We looked. There lay a woman’s body half-buried in garbage.  
‘Why drag her?’ Peter grumbled. ‘Just a drunk whore.’  

Yeshu’s hand shot out, gripping Peter’s clothing with surprising force. The pain vanished from his eyes for a moment. He tried to speak, shuddered all over, and passed out again.  
Jan glared murderously at Peter. Peter jutted his lip. Yakov and I rolled up our sleeves and headed for the woman.

### Chapter 6: FRACTURE FIELD

Ever since Mary moved in with us, I couldn’t think of anything else.  
That half-witted creature with eyes black as night had hijacked my mind completely.  
Every free minute belonged to her, though I had never once spoken to her. Didn’t need to. Thinking was enough.

Mary, I kept repeating to myself. Mary.  
Poor street hawker from the edge of town. God’s own simpleton — so simple the word “godly” fits her like a glove.

We’re so proud of our ability to think. We love to philosophize. We puff out our cheeks, scratch our foreheads, build theories.  
But you, Mary — we look up at you.  
From below.

What’s your secret, you divine creature?  
Is it your clumsy ugliness?  
Or the way you obey every whim without a word?  
The way you quietly lift your skirt when our flesh demands it?

Why do I say “our”?  
Yakov. Peter. Andrew. Jan. Even all-knowing Yeshu — they’ve all had you, Mary.  
But not me.  
I’m afraid you’d let me too. That you’d lift your skirt for me just as silently. And then I’d be no different from the rest of them.  
Though maybe I’m not different at all…  
Still, I want you to think I am.

Yeshu pretends he doesn’t notice how they use you. And why should he? He has his teaching, and he’s loyal to it like a dog to its master.

We sit at the table eating.  
Jan tears into a lamb leg like a wild animal. Peter pokes his rice with disgust.  
You sit quietly in the corner. I don’t even know if you’re eating or drinking — my eyes are somewhere else.  
I pretend to listen to Yeshu. I nod when everyone nods. I laugh when they laugh. I raise my glass when they raise theirs. I make toasts when asked.  
But not a hundredth part of my heart is in any of it.  
And my soul… my soul isn’t there at all.

I think of you when I brush my teeth.  
I think of you when I’m falling asleep.  
I think of you every market morning, twice a week, when I drag myself out to buy vegetables and fruit.

“How much?” I ask the cheerful vendor, pointing at the tomatoes.  
He tears himself away from chatting with another seller and names the price. I start filling my basket.

Then I freeze. The vendors are talking about Yeshu. Nothing unusual — he preaches more and more these days. But this time it’s not about the sermons.  
It’s about Yeshu and Mary.

“Kill me if you must, Moshe,” says the jolly vendor, “but I can’t remember his name. I remember him talking about the lonely soul and the beard. But the name…”  
“They called him Yeshu, Yeshu,” the other replies. “What does it matter…”  
The jolly one nods.  
“Word is some Mary — Shlomo’s daughter — joined their gang. You know him?”  
“Nope. But she’s gonna catch hell when old Shlomo finds out.”  
“He’s known for ages, Moshe.” The vendor scratches his hanging belly. “Says he has no daughter anymore.”  
“Fair enough. And her?”  
“Nothing. Just heard her name’s Mary. Washed some beggar Jew’s feet.”  
“Heard he’s not a beggar at all — some rich native from Australia. Black. Sweaty. Smells like kangaroo shit.”  

They howl with laughter.  
“Likes walking on water!” Moshe grins. “Maybe he floats because he’s part dung?”  
“Forgive me, forgive me!” the jolly vendor clutches his chest. “Feeds a thousand with two fish — classic Jew trick!”  
“A real model of the tribe.”  
“Yeah, I’ll ask him to buy me a Rolls-Royce on my salary!”  
“Ask away,” Moshe nods, “but watch out! The scammer takes a cut of every deal.”  
“A cut? The leather driver’s seat?”  
“No — the exhaust pipe. Looks like the swollen hemorrhoids of a provincial queer.”  

Another wave of laughter.

I pay and leave.  
I walk through the city listening — everyone’s talking about the Teacher. Sometimes they recognize me and swarm with questions. I slip away down side streets.  

Mary…  
I’m so fucking tired of all this.  
Soon I’ll be home again… playing my filthy role again.  
I’m so tired, Mary…

By the time I reach the house, it’s already dark.

### Chapter 7: THE EYE THAT FORGETS

Savage cursing echoed from the entry. Not Yakov this time.

Mary tried to slip off Yeshu’s lap — he held her still.

“Another visitor,” he said.

“The same one?” Jan asked.

“Exactly,” Yeshu nodded.

“A lover of… full-bodied women.”

Mary looked drained as hell.

“…No, you don’t get it! She’s a Madonna! I found her in some Siberian backwater — bella mia! I painted her night after night — I’d have painted her forever!”

A painter burst in — eyes wild — then froze when he saw Peter.

“I imagined you… differently,” he muttered.

Peter flushed deep red.

For a second, I almost pitied the artist.

He circled slowly, sizing us up. When his eyes met mine — a pause.  
Then he frowned.  
And looked away.

“Problem?” I asked. “Name?”

“Leo,” he snapped.

“So what’s your problem, Leo?”

“No problem, señor.”

“Still,” Yeshu said, “you seem rattled. Speak.”

Leo’s eyes softened toward him.

“I didn’t expect him to be here.”

He pointed at me.

I snorted.

“Déjà vu.”

“Dear Leo,” Yeshu smiled. “Why do guests from the future keep obsessing over my disciple?”

Leo sighed.

“Better you didn’t know.”

“As you wish.”

Yeshu glanced at me. It stuck.

Everyone followed.

Peter, thrilled to be off the hook, grinned. Jan blinked, lost. Yakov frowned.

Mary’s eyes darkened — her fragile balance starting to crack.

I lit a cigarette.

“What’re you staring at? Your mothers…”

“Yes! Yes!” Leo cut in, flustered. “Nonsense — ignore it. Look!”

He waved a sketch: some wide-faced village girl.

“Lovely,” Yeshu said, not looking. “So, Leo… why are you here?”

Leo deflated — then caught himself.

“Señor, I paint. Such people… such faces… They mustn’t be lost.”

“You want to paint us?”

“I do, señor.”

“Then go ahead. We’re yours.”

Yeshu poured the wine.

Leo started sketching.

We sat in silence — each lost in our own dread. Life without Yeshu was unthinkable.

Suddenly Leo crumpled the paper.

“No! I can’t. There’s no unity in you — none!”

Thank God, I thought. Unity is the last thing we need.

Suddenly — a stranger stood in the doorway.

“I’m Alexey Dubrovsky,” he said quietly. “Someone here left their last line unsung.”

He vanished. Left behind the scent of tobacco — and the faint twang of a drawn string.
